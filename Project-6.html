Turf: Parking for Vibrant Community

Overview

As self-driving cars will be the next wave in changing the ecosystem in the shared economy and city planning within the districts, we found that this trend will decrease the needs to have parking spaces and parking kiosks in the communities. From the interview with Director Parking and Metering of Pittsburgh, we got the insights on how most of the city government revenue is from parking fees, and these fees also go into the city for public services (i.e., street maintenance, utilities and environmental maintenance).Since this is a 2-week design sprint project, we narrow down our concepts to design for a vibrant communitywhich create an intuitive system by connecting local businesses into the parking ecosystem.For instance, if Joe parks and shops in the district, he could get the rebate for the parking fee and see the visualization on how his contribution goes into public service. As the transition with mixed of self-driving cars and regular cars in 5-7 years, we envision that this system will assist people and local businesses that are underprivileged in the future.

Process

Primary Research

We applied five research methods in our primary research:Interview, expert interview, contextual inquiry, shadowing and an online survey. We conducted 30 people in both survey and interviews, and we found out three main pain-points from the residents in Pittsburgh. The pain-points are parking spots in downtown is hard to find, parking fare is expensive, and kiosk interface is confusing for elders. For applying both contextual inquiries and shadowing methods to parking issues, we observed how residents dealt with the problems and how frustrated they feel during the process.

Interviews

As we were looking into some of the pain-points that we conducted in our interviews, we decided to move on for expert interviews. We got valuable insights about self-driving cars from Jodi Forlizzi, HCI professor at CMU, said that autonomous cars would come like a tsunami and designers need to work with city planners to restructure the ecosystem in urban planning. She worried about the transition time where mixed of autonomous cars and regular cars. We also talk to John Fournier, director at Parking & Metering in the City of Pittsburgh. He mentioned that parking fees are so important for the city government since they are one of the main revenues for the city and people is not aware how these fees are also for maintaining utilization. He said that street parking also creates a barrier for pedestrians from traffic.It creates a sense of safety. Our research shows that fewer accidents happen in these areas.

Territory Map


As we identified the problem that we wanted to address, we wanted to narrow down to a group of stakeholders that will fit our concepts. We analyzed the directed and undirected stakeholders and their relationship between the pain-points we would like to address. We found that our interests land on residents, street-parking drivers, and local businesses. We also found that they had two things in common, safety and vibrant community.




User Journey


We also got insights on our user journey map and the persona we created. Our persona is Joe, a 28 years-old accountant living in the suburbs in Pittsburgh area. He needs to commute to Downtown Pittsburgh to work every day. He stressed about find parking spots on the street and anxious about getting tickets for not extending time. Then we mapped out Joe's commute journey from driving to work to pick up his car after work. We analyzed the pain-points in relation to his key moments and how these factors affect his stress level during the process. From there, we found opportunities to design for interventions for those pain-points. For instance, Joe needs to walk back to park spot to extend time, and he is stressful about extending parking time at work. We saw an opportunity to use we-link for the user to extend time. This gives flexibility to other users who don't want to download an app.

    Wireframes


    Since we were designing for the future look of the kiosk and how the system interconnected with the local businesses and user contributions. Users will see a menu for five options (pay to park, explore the neighborhoods, find my car, validate my ticket and real-time traffic). The kiosk system also syncs with the mobile web version so users could extend parking time without downloading an app. We want to design an intuitive and smooth flow that would upgrade the current parking system in Pittsburgh. By integrating QR code to collect for rebating parking fees and visualization of contribution for public service, we encourage citizens to participate in making their community more vibrant in the future. 3

    User Flow


    The user, a driver, in this context, should quickly select parking time and go through the payment. Seamlessly send the receipt on the mobile phone and effortlessly extend time on the mobile without walking back to the parking spot. Also, by scanning QR code while shopping in the local businesses, will not only get discount from parking fee but also help the businesses to grow in the district. Lastly, by rebating user's parking fee, the user will visualize his contribution to the public service in the community. The interface of the parking kiosk and mobile web version have to be user-friendly and easy to understand so that the user will have a stress-free time when he is visiting the business district.

    Design

    Connecting parking system with local businesses to design for vibrant community

    Unlike the current parking kiosk, Turf extends the kiosk usage by adding features such as exploring neighborhoods, finding my car, notifying real-time traffic and validating my ticket. We leverage the QR code on parking receipt for parking time extension and collecting rebate for the parking fee. This not only solves the pain-points of drivers but also promote local businesses in the district. The new and fresh interface is intuitive for everyone, especially with elders who confused with current parking kiosk system.

    Extending parking time becomes stressless with Turf mobile web version

    By scanning QR code on the parking receipt, it will take users to the Turf web version on mobile devices. One of the pain-point for the visitors is that they do not want to download an app just for extending parking time. Turf mobile web version gives more flexibility for both local and visitors.

    Incorporate playful motion design to the future kiosk system

    We design fun motion design to provide explicit instruction when users are going through each step. We also design for inclusive that foreigners and less literate people could understand clearly as well.

    Bridge gaps between visitors and the community by designing visualization for citizens to understand their contribution to the public services.

    Users will get parking fee discount by scanning their purchases through QR code receipt in the local businesses, and they will get discount on parking fee. We use this incentive as a motivation for people to park and shop around in the local stores. As they rebate discount at the kiosk, our data visualization will show citizen's contribution to the public services. We hope to bring awareness to citizens that these fees go into refining a better community in return for citizen's contribution.

    Future kiosk design

    We design a fresh and friendly look for the future kiosk. There are a QR code scanner and NFC reader for Apply Pay / Andriod Pay and touchscreen in color interface. We also integrate wheelchair assistance into our kiosk design for people in wheelchair quickly access to the touchscreen.
